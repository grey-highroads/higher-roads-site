2Experience Design & Content Scope
What the creative team is actually building and how long it takes.
3Interactivity & Trigger Logic
Highest-risk area for scope creep. Ask whether the interactivity needs to be real or just needs to feel real.
Higher Roads take: gesture tracking adds calibration burden, failure risk, and cost. Scripted presence-sensor logic with well-paced media achieves the same perceived responsiveness at a fraction of the risk.
4Personalization & Real-Time Systems
Any element that changes per visitor is a systems integration project, not pre-rendered media.
Internal rule: do not scope or price the real-time rendering layer without a joint session with the technical team responsible for intake and session handling.
5Photo & Media Capture
Frequently mentioned as a deliverable, rarely fully scoped upfront.
Higher Roads take: never describe a specific camera trigger mechanism to a client without flagging it as assumed if it hasn't been verified by whoever is actually building it.
6Hardware
Rent vs. buy: single event, rental may be fine. Multi-stop tour, purchase is almost always better — owned gear travels with the installation and the cost is fixed regardless of stop count. Purchase is a capital authorization decision, not a pricing decision.
7Safety & Compliance
Rarely surfaces until it blocks load-in. Worth a decision either way rather than a silent gap.
8Installation, Calibration & Execution
Higher Roads take: on-site calibration and technical integration are the same work by the same people in sequence. Price as one implementation block, not two separate lines.
9Duration & Tour Structure
Higher Roads take: content cost does not repeat across locations. Implementation labor does. Equipment cost scales with duration regardless of rent or own. Always quote single-event and full-run as separate scenarios.
10Scope Boundaries & Third-Party Dependencies
Scope ambiguity is where margin goes to die.
